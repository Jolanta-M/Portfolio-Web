Problem and Objective

According to the Baymard Institute research - the global average of the online shopping cart abandonment is currently 70.19% despite of the investments into e-commerce websites. During the fourteen years of usability testing, they found the main cause for this to be the design and flow of the checkout. (Baymard, 2026) And with the global e-commerce statistics showing that online sales reached $6 trillion in 2024 and are projected to grow by 31% in upcoming years (Aviso, 2025) it is very compelling for e-commerce retailers to ‘get it right’ with the UX of the online shopping cart.

That’s why in this project for a fictional online Tea Shop I focused on designing a shopping cart experience that is complying with the research-based checkout flows and established UX design guidelines which are proven to reduce user drop-off levels and minimise user frustration. I also made sure that the cart has an original and appealing UI design.

Design Approach

In this project for the fictional desktop-focused Tea Shop website, I used research driven UX approach to design an original shopping cart experience aligned with proven guidelines that reduce user drop-off. I also relied on Udemy UX/UI design course of Andrei Neagoie and Daniel Schifano which followed established Usability Heuristics and User-Centred design. Figma was the tool I used to create the project. Additionally, I used AI Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Microsoft Copilot to ideate on the design decisions, look for relevant UX research and to create project-specific images.

I was working according to Agile methodology and that always made each step of the project flexible for changes, but the main pattern of the design process was:

Design decision walkthrough for online Shopping Cart

Cart abandonment has multiple causes — disorientation, user’s psychological guilt, anxiety about mistakes, friction, distraction, etc. The following is a design decision walkthrough which targets many of those causes. It includes high-fidelity wireframes with annotations and references to the research.

1. Mitigating the cart abandonment by reducing user’s guilt

According to Oxford Academic research on shopping cart abandonment - many consumers treat the shopping cart as the virtual storage space for items they are considering buying and during the purchase psychological drivers like perceptions of product hedonism and consumer guilt play the role. This research suggests that “purchasing carts with a higher hedonic-to-utilitarian product ratio induces stronger guilt. Consequently, this increases the likelihood of cart abandonment.” (Hadar et al, 2026)
Researchers advise that recommending the utilitarian products can shift user’s perception of the cart’s general hedonic value and reduce the user guilt. Those utilitarian items don’t even have to be added to the cart to reframe user’s perception of it.

In this case study of an online Tea Shop - the specialty, loose-leaf tea can be perceived as indulgent, that’s why this design approach seems reasonable: below the cart the utilitarian items are recommended, such as: brewing equipment, tea storage boxes, everyday teabags, descaler sachets for kettles, reusable travel cups/flasks, etc.

Furthermore, the same design approach is maintained in the cart overlay popup. After user adds the item to the cart -they see a popup with the relevant information as well as visually secondary cross-sells of utilitarian items.

Liat Hadar, Yael Steinhart, Gil Appel, Yaniv Shani (2026) The Effect of Online Cart Composition on Cart Abandonment, Journal of Consumer Research.
Available at : https://doi.org/10.1093/jcr/ucag002

2. Making the checkout process steps clear and visible

When users are navigating multistep processes, they feel disoriented and hesitate if it’s not clear how long it will take to complete. That’s why it’s important to provide visual steps of the whole process and avoid combining them (Baymard, 2026) In the design below I used the progress bar to clearly communicate process steps. Progress bar also serves as navigation to move forward or back in addition to main button.

Baymard Institute (2026) Always Provide Accurate Process Steps for Multistep Processes #611. Available at : #611: Always Provide Accurate Process Steps for Multistep Processes – General Form Design & Checkout Layout – Cart & Checkout – Research Catalog – Baymard Premium

3. Reducing Distractions During Checkout

Following the Baymard Institute checkout usability research guidelines I made navigational distractions like cross sells to be placed as visually secondary content on the ‘Added to cart’ confirmation overlay so that user would not lose focus on task completion. The same principle was followed in the cart page, where the recommended items are displayed as secondary content – at the bottom of the page.

Baymard Institute (2026) Ensure Cross-Sells in “Added to Cart” Overlays Are Styled as Secondary Content #600. Available at : #600: Ensure Cross-Sells in “Added to Cart” Overlays Are Styled as Secondary Content – Shopping Cart – Cart & Checkout – Research Catalog – Baymard Premium

4. Ensuring cart items are visible and adjustable

I followed the Nielsen Norman Group’s advice about providing user access to a full shopping cart where they can see all or most of added items at once. Even though the default design of my cart is closer to a minicart, when the user adds more than two items - they can see the ‘expand’ icon on the cart and after pressing it - enter the full cart view. This combination of minicart and optional full cart display gives user the freedom to choose according to their preferences.

I also made sure the user can modify items in the cart- change their quantity, grams or delete them if they want. Items in the cart have clear image, price and short product description. Also, details on total amount and total number of items in the cart are displayed. This information is clear and always visible on both minicart and full cart.

Schade A. (2014) Decision Making in the Ecommerce Shopping Cart: 4 Tips for Supporting User, Nielsen Norman Group. Available at : Decision Making in the Ecommerce Shopping Cart: 4 Tips For Supporting Users - NN/G

5. Visible and immediate user feedback

The Nielsen Norman Group suggests providing user with visual feedback after any action which has consequences to user. This adheres to the Nielsen’s usability heuristics rules, mainly visibility of system status and error prevention. Timely feedback gives user the feeling of control, clarity, reduces uncertainty, gives them chance to identify and fix errors and in this way increases user trust to the brand and provides better experience. This design have:

  • Menu cart icon which updates visually with colour change and item count
  • Confirmation overlay popup when user adds item to the cart
  • The success message displayed after product purchase
  • The logged-in user can see the updated price and discounted amount immediately after redeeming their points
  • Every button has hover effect
  • Cart related information such as totals is updated immediately
  • Loading icon is displayed if user must wait for the page to load
  • Expand / Collapse icon becomes available if cart holds more than two items
  • Checkout Progress bar with distinct visual states per step
  • Menu icon of account is updated with colour after user is logged-in
I also made sure the user can modify items in the cart- change their quantity, grams or delete them if they want. Items in the cart have clear image, price and short product description. Also, details on total amount and total number of items in the cart are displayed. This information is clear and always visible on both minicart and full cart.

Harley A. (2018) Visibility of system status (Usability Heuristic #1), Nielsen Norman Group. Available at : Visibility of System Status - NN/G
Laubheimer P. (2018)Adding an Item to a Shopping Cart: Provide Clear, Persistent Feedback, Nielsen Norman Group. Available at : Adding an Item to a Shopping Cart: Provide Clear, Persistent Feedback - NN/G

6. Multiple third-party payment options

One of the UX issues marked by Baymard Institute is that missing payment options are the cause for cart abandonments. That’s why they suggest to always support one or more third-party payment options.

In this project in addition to Card payment, I included two other third-party payment options, mainly Google Pay and Apple Pay. I also used official branded Google and Apple Pay buttons as the trigger for the payment flow which is compliant with Google and Apple policies. The size of the buttons and their look was adjusted in compliance with the Google and Apple official design guidelines and with locked aspect ratio (Apple Pay: height min 32px, weight min 175px / Google Pay: height recommendation 48px, width min 152px for ‘Buy with Google Pay’ button). In my design - Google Pay button: height 40px, width 283px. Apple Pay button: height 40px, width 274.

7. Guest user checkout as default option

Nielsen Norman Group as well as many other reputable UX research institutions reveal that e-commerce usability research suggests creating guest checkout as default option instead of forcing users to register with website to be able to make purchase. Many users plan only a one-time purchase or do not want to go with additional steps to create an account, or do not want to share their personal details with website. Whatever the reason, if user has no option to buy as a guest - this directly affects the level of cart dropout.

For this project – Tea Shop website has the Guest checkout as default option and other options are the suggestions to either create an account or to login if it exists. After the purchase - on the success message page user is informed about earned loyalty points and encouraged to create an account with only one simple step (personal user details were already provided). The registration is shown as beneficial to the user, who will be able to save the loyalty points and use them for future discounts. Research suggests that this approach finds the best response from users.

Schade A. (2015) Don’t Force Users to Register Before They Can Buy. Available at : Don't Force Users to Register Before They Can Buy - NN/G

8. Order Summary step optimization

According to Baymard Institute issues encountered by users in the Order Review step (in this project- Order Summary step) can cause user cart abandonment, even this late in checkout process. To mitigate this, I implemented suggested optimisations for this step:

  • Allow users to edit errors directly in Order Summary page – to avoid the necessity for the user to go back into previous steps, which can be frustrating.
  • Have Edit links close to each semantic group – user must clearly see the option to Edit needed field inside the Order Summary to avoid going back to previous steps.
  • Provide a ‘Place Order’ button (here - ‘Pay Now’ button) in the way user can spot it - to avoid user misunderstanding this page as Confirmation instead of Order Summary page. Otherwise, the user might think they already placed the order when they did not.
  • Optimize this step for quick visual skimming - Order Summary step should not waste user’s focus on non-order information
  • Relevant information displayed at Order Summary step – delivery address broken into multiple lines for scannability, payment details with last four digits, email, autoformatted user’s phone number (in this project – not included), delivery estimations in date format instead of speed format, cost breakdown with Total Price the user will actually pay as visually dominant, product quantities and thumbnails.

Scott E. (2019) 5 ‘Order Review’ UX Implementations That Make Office Depot Best-in-Class. Baymard Institute. Available at : 5 ‘Order Review’ UX Implementations That Make Office Depot Best-in-Class – Baymard

9. UX design patterns for payment form

According to Baymard Institute there are clear UX steps that help reduce cart abandonment and user frustration during payment form completion. In this project I want to highlight some of them:

  • Data retention in sensitive Credit Card fields – reduces friction by keeping user’s previously entered Card information so they don’t have to retype it again after an error.
  • Autoformatting spaces in the Credit Card number field - matching how the numbers appear on the physical card to improve user input accuracy.
  • Saving "Guest" user’s previously entered Credit Card information – compliance with PCI DSS regulations on how card data can be stored and prompting user to agree to save their data.
  • Credit Card information validation – providing user with validation feedback and showing clear error messages.

Soderlund L. (2017) The ‘Credit Card Number’ Field Must Allow and Auto-Format Spaces (80% Don’t). Baymard Institute. Available at : Credit Card Number Field: Auto-Format Spaces - Baymard – Baymard Premium
